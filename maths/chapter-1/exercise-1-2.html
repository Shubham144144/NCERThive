What is an Irrational Number? A number that cannot be written as p/q, where p and q are integers and q is not 0, is called irrational. Examples: √2, √3, √5, π.

Method Used: Proof by Contradiction. We assume the number IS rational. Then we show this leads to a contradiction. So our assumption was wrong. Hence the number is irrational.

Question 1

Question:

Prove that √5 is irrational.

Solution:

Step 1: Assume the opposite.

Let us assume that √5 is rational.

So √5 = p/q, where p and q are integers, q is not 0, and HCF(p, q) = 1 (no common factor).

Step 2: Square both sides.

5 = p²/q²

So p² = 5q² ... (1)

Step 3: 5 divides p.

From (1), 5 divides p².

By the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, if 5 divides p², then 5 divides p.

So let p = 5m for some integer m.

Step 4: Substitute p = 5m in (1).

(5m)² = 5q²

25m² = 5q²

q² = 5m² ... (2)

Step 5: 5 divides q.

From (2), 5 divides q².

So 5 divides q as well.

Step 6: Contradiction.

5 divides both p and q. So HCF(p, q) is at least 5.

But we said HCF(p, q) = 1. This is a contradiction!

Answer: Our assumption is wrong. Therefore, √5 is irrational. (Proved)

Question 2

Question:

Prove that 3 + 2√5 is irrational.

Solution:

Step 1: Assume the opposite.

Let us assume that 3 + 2√5 is rational.

So 3 + 2√5 = p/q, where p and q are integers and q is not 0.

Step 2: Rearrange to isolate √5.

2√5 = p/q – 3

2√5 = (p – 3q)/q

√5 = (p – 3q)/2q

Step 3: Right side is rational.

Since p, q, 3, 2 are all integers, (p – 3q)/2q is a rational number.

This means √5 is rational.

Step 4: Contradiction.

But we proved in Question 1 that √5 is irrational.

Rational cannot equal irrational. This is a contradiction!

Answer: Our assumption is wrong. Therefore, 3 + 2√5 is irrational. (Proved)

Question 3

Question:

Prove that the following are irrationals:

(i) 1/√2    (ii) 7√5    (iii) 6 + √2

Solution (i): Prove 1/√2 is irrational

Step 1: Assume the opposite.

Let 1/√2 = p/q, where p and q are integers and q is not 0.

Step 2: Rearrange.

√2 = q/p

Step 3: Right side is rational.

q/p is rational since p and q are integers.

So √2 is rational.

Step 4: Contradiction.

But √2 is irrational (proved in NCERT textbook). Contradiction!

Answer: Therefore, 1/√2 is irrational. (Proved)

Solution (ii): Prove 7√5 is irrational

Step 1: Assume the opposite.

Let 7√5 = p/q, where p and q are integers and q is not 0.

Step 2: Rearrange.

√5 = p/7q

Step 3: Right side is rational.

p/7q is rational since p, 7, q are all integers.

So √5 is rational.

Step 4: Contradiction.

But √5 is irrational (proved in Question 1). Contradiction!

Answer: Therefore, 7√5 is irrational. (Proved)

Solution (iii): Prove 6 + √2 is irrational

Step 1: Assume the opposite.

Let 6 + √2 = p/q, where p and q are integers and q is not 0.

Step 2: Rearrange to isolate √2.

√2 = p/q – 6

√2 = (p – 6q)/q

Step 3: Right side is rational.

(p – 6q)/q is rational since p, q, 6 are all integers.

So √2 is rational.

Step 4: Contradiction.

But √2 is irrational. Contradiction!

Answer: Therefore, 6 + √2 is irrational. (Proved)

Key Points to Remember – Exercise 1.2

  • Irrational numbers cannot be written in p/q form.
  • Always use Proof by Contradiction method.
  • Key rule: If prime p divides a², then p divides a.
  • √2, √3, √5 are all irrational — must remember.
  • rational + irrational = irrational always.
  • rational × irrational = irrational always.
  • Write all steps clearly in board exam — each step carries marks.
